## Aristotle
- provided the first systematic written treatment of logic
- it was close to [[Propositional Logic]], but it was informal and symbolic.
## Leibniz
- 2000 years after Aristotle, he built upon his work.
- He wanted to create a symbolic language of reasoning that could be used to solve all well-defined problems, not just in mathematics. 
## Augustus De Morgan , George  Boole
- De Morgan is best known for the two laws of propositional logic that bear his name.
- Boolean algebra found many uses in the 20th century, in design principles of computer circuitry.
## Gottlob Fegre
- He created [[First Order Logic]] in the latter part of 1800s
- He gave the first rigorous treatment of the logic of quantifiers.
- He applied his work to create formal theories of arithmetic.
## Peano
- He also created [[First Order Logic]] independently, a few years after Fegre
- He invented a lot of symbols currently used in propositional logic and set theory.
- He also applied his work to create formal theories of arithmetic.
- His theory found wide acceptance, but Frege's work had more influence on the development of Modern Mathematical logic.

## Bertrand Russel and Alfred North Whitehead
- They both led the logicist program , in which they wanted to free mathematics from contradictions that were discovered in set theory at the end of 19th century. 
- They both extended the work of Fegre and Peano.
- They wrote the 2000 page *Principia Mathematica*, which took a decade to write, and attempted to write all of Mathematics in terms of (just) logic.
- However, they did not quite succeed in this, as mathematics cannot be based solely on logic. They provided much of the groundwork for the formalization of Mathematics within set theory.

## Hilbert
- He led the **formalist** program, in which he wanted to put all of Mathematics into a completely symbolic axiomatic framework, and then use metamathematics (mathematics applied to mathematics , also called proof theory) to show that the mathematics is free from contradictions.
- Two major parts of Hilbert's program -- the translation of mathematical logic and mathematical proofs into a purely formal, symbolic format has exceeded very well.
